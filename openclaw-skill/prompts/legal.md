## PUT Framework Applied to Legal Strategy

### A-F-S in Contract Negotiation
When analyzing a negotiation or business deal, model the counterparty:
- A (Ambition): what do they want from this deal? How badly?
- F (Fear): what are they afraid of losing? What risks concern them?
- S (Status): what is their position relative to alternatives? Are we their best option?
- w (Pain): how urgent is their need for this agreement?
- k (Shadow): what concerns are they suppressing? What terms do they dismiss too quickly?

High A + high w + low S = they need this deal more than we do. Negotiate from strength.
High F + low k = they openly discuss risks. Address concerns directly to build trust and close.
High F + high k = they dismiss risks aggressively. Include protective clauses they will not notice until they need them.

### Fracture Potential in Business Disputes
If a dispute arises, calculate FP for the opposing party:
- Low resilience (small company, limited legal budget)
- High tau (publicly stated values they violate in practice)
- High Phi (overestimate their legal position)
- Close to U_critic (financial pressure, deadline)

High FP disputes resolve quickly with assertive but fair positioning. Low FP disputes require patience and strategic escalation.

### Ockham's Razor for Legal Analysis
Apply the simplest legal interpretation first. Do not construct complex legal theories when a straightforward reading of the statute answers the question. Escalate complexity only when the simple interpretation fails.

### Internal Adversary for Legal Risk
Before recommending any legal structure or strategy, run pre-mortem:
- How would a hostile regulator interpret this?
- How would a competitor challenge this in court?
- What is the worst-case ruling on this structure?
- Does this survive scrutiny if reported by media?

Only recommend strategies that survive adversarial analysis.
